Why We Exist

Psychology without borders.

Most student academic communities study psychology from a single national perspective. We study it from forty. Criminal behavior, mental illness, patient experience, and human psychology all look fundamentally different depending on the legal system, healthcare infrastructure, cultural values, and historical context of the country you are in.

We believe studying these differences together — as students who actually live inside those different systems — produces richer, more honest, and more meaningful academic work.

About

Built for students who want
to do real work.

The Psychology Collective is a structured, student-led academic community for students who are seriously interested in psychology. Through original research, long-term projects, discussions, and a quarterly journal, members explore psychological issues from cross-cultural and interdisciplinary perspectives.

Every member contributes to published work, whether through research, writing, editing, design, event planning, or project development. Our fully online and asynchronous structure allows students from over 30 countries to participate meaningfully without mandatory live meetings.

Our goal is for The Psychology Collective to be recognized as more than an extracurricular club: a platform where students build credible academic experience, contribute to a global psychology community, and produce work they can confidently reference in future applications, portfolios, and CVs.

Founder's Note

When I first created The Psychology Collective, I wanted it to be more than a place where students shared a common interest in psychology. I wanted it to become a space where students could think seriously, write carefully, and contribute to genuine academic discussions about the human mind and behaviour.

Our first issue, What Shapes Human Behaviour?, reflects that intention. Human behaviour is never the product of a single cause. The way people think, feel, and act emerges from a complex interplay of biology, psychology, culture, upbringing, environment, and social systems. Any decision, emotion, belief, or action can only be fully understood when we examine the many forces that shape it.

This theme was chosen because it gives our contributors the freedom to approach psychology from many different angles. Some examine the brain, hormones, or personality. Others write about culture, morality, childhood, social influence, mental health, or criminal behaviour. Together, these perspectives demonstrate that psychology is not a single field with a single answer — it is a discipline that asks us to look deeper, question our assumptions, and understand people with greater nuance.

As our first publication, this issue also marks the beginning of The Psychology Collective as a global student-led academic community. With members from over 30 countries, we hope to build a platform where students learn from one another's perspectives and produce work that reflects real curiosity, careful research, and genuine effort.

I am proud of everyone who contributed to this issue. Whether through research, writing, editing, design, or coordination, each member helped create something larger than any individual project. This journal is not simply a collection of essays — it is evidence of what students can accomplish when they are given the space to explore ideas seriously and collaboratively.

I hope this first issue invites readers to think more carefully about the forces that shape human behaviour — not only in others, but in themselves.

Jeeyoo KimFounder, The Psychology Collective · Issue 01 · 2026

Issue Theme

Theme confirmed · Drafts in progress

What Shapes Human Behaviour?

This theme explores the wide range of factors that influence how people think, feel, and act. Human behaviour is shaped by a complex interaction between biological processes, psychological mechanisms, and social environments. Contributors may examine how internal factors such as brain function, hormones, and personality interact with external influences like culture, upbringing, and societal systems.

This theme also allows for exploration of behaviour in different contexts — from everyday decision-making to more complex issues such as crime, morality, and social influence. Writers can approach it from multiple perspectives, including clinical, cognitive, social, and biological psychology, making it one of the most flexible and inclusive themes for cross-cultural academic work.

Come ready
to contribute.

The Psychology Collective welcomes ambitious high school students from any country and any subfield. We are not looking for perfect grades — we are looking for students who are genuinely curious and ready to engage seriously with psychology across cultures.

Apply Now

Applications reviewed on a rolling basis · Fully online · Asynchronous

Who we're looking for

Open to high school students from anywhere in the world with an interest in psychology — any subfield, any academic background, any country. We do not require prior research experience.

What we do require is genuine curiosity, a willingness to engage with psychology across cultural contexts, and the commitment to complete your work reliably and on time.

What to expect

  • One original research analysis per 10-week cycle
  • Participation in biweekly asynchronous discussions
  • Collaboration with 60+ members across 30+ countries
  • Your work published in a publicly archived journal
  • A publication you can cite on university applications
  • No mandatory live meetings — fully asynchronous

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need prior research experience?

No. We will guide you through the research and writing process. What matters is that you engage genuinely with your topic and are willing to learn.

How much time does membership require?

Roughly 3–5 hours per week during active writing periods, and less during discussion-only weeks. All deadlines are designed with global time zones in mind.

Can I join if English is not my first language?

Yes. Research Editors will help non-native English speakers with language clarity. The important thing is the quality of your thinking and research — not perfect English.

Which division will I be in?

You can indicate a preference when you apply. Placement is based on your skills, interests, and what the community needs at the time of your application.
